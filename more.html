Different Bodies

DIFFIRENT BODIES OF WATER FOR KIDS

Given that more than 70 percent of the Earth's surface is covered by water, it is crucial to have knowledge about the diverse array of water bodies present on the globe. This encompasses both freshwater and saltwater aquatic environments.

Water can be found in various forms and bodies, ranging from little, flowing streams to expansive, profound oceans. Each type and body of water possesses distinct characteristics, dimensions, and organisms that inhabit it.

Running Rivers

Streams, alternatively referred to as "brooks" or "creeks," are typically unobstructed waterways that descend due to the force of gravity. Streams converge with other water bodies, including neighboring streams, lakes, or the ocean. Watersheds are formed by the hills that separate them.

River water originates from precipitation runoff, groundwater springs, or the local aquifer. A stream can have a maximum length of 99 miles, whereas rivers have a length exceeding 100 miles. Bodies of water such as rivers and streams are invariably encompassed by terrestrial areas. Almost all streams consist of freshwater.

Abundant Aquifers

An aquifer refers to a layer of rock or soil beneath the surface that is completely filled with water, often referred to as a "underground river."

The majority of rivers and streams are linked to aquifers at their origin, along with numerous lakes. Aquifers typically contain freshwater, however they may acquire a slight salinity due to the underlying geological formations. Their replenishment occurs through the infiltration of rainwater into the ground.

Landlocked Lakes

Lakes and ponds are completely enclosed by land. These bodies of water are frequently nourished by streams or rivers, and they can also originate from a spring connected to an underground water source known as an aquifer. Lakes, similar to rivers, can also provide potable water to adjacent urban areas.

The majority of lakes are comprised of freshwater, with the notable exception of the Great Salt Lake in Utah.

Salty Seas

Defining seas can be challenging due to their ability to exist either as bodies of water enclosed by land, similar to lakes, or as extensions of the ocean, such as gulfs. Geographers categorize seas into three classifications: nearly enclosed seas, partly enclosed seas, and hypersaline lakes. Every ocean contains a high concentration of salt.

Almost landlocked bodies of water separate land masses within continents and are linked to the ocean, like the Mediterranean Sea. Partially contained seas, such as the Weddell Sea of Antarctica, resemble gulfs and are accessible to the ocean. Hypersaline lakes are landlocked bodies of water that have a high concentration of salt, like the Dead Sea.

The Open Ocean

The ocean is the most extensive aquatic expanse on Earth, characterized by its boundless nature. While we assign distinct names to various oceanic regions such as the Pacific, Atlantic, Arctic, Indian, and Southern, they are essentially interconnected and form a single, uninterrupted expanse of water.

The waters of the Earth are interconnected with the oceans, forming a vast and global hydrological system. The ocean is comprised of saline water and encompasses 97 percent of the Earth's water supply.

A vast majority of marine life avoids the open ocean. Nevertheless, certain species of whales, sizable fish, and sharks occasionally traverse these waters for purposes such as migration, mating, or feeding.
